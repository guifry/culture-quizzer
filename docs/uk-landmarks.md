angles (When / Who / People / Events / Concepts) → 4–5 clues → concept keys**. Concepts are
defined once in the shared [Glossary](#glossary) and reused across entries.

- ★ = Must-Know 10 (cosmetic cue only; identical treatment to the rest).
- Clues are each self-sufficient (a random one appears alone) and each takes a different angle.
- The course is a superset of the clues — it carries knowledge the clue mode never tests
  (reserved for a future LLM-graded deep mode).

## 1 · Palace of Westminster & Big Ben ★
*England · City of Westminster, London*

**Nutshell.** The seat of the UK Parliament on the north bank of the Thames — a vast neo-Gothic
palace rebuilt after a fire in 1834, its clock tower (the Elizabeth Tower) housing the great bell
nicknamed Big Ben. It is the most recognised silhouette of Britain.

- **When** — Royal palace from the 11th C; present building 1840–1876, in the Gothic Revival.
- **Who** — A royal residence until 1512, then home to Parliament — the seat of British democracy,
  Commons and Lords.
- **People** — Architects Charles Barry and Augustus Pugin. "Big Ben" is the bell, not the tower,
  probably named after Sir Benjamin Hall. Guy Fawkes tried to destroy it in 1605.
- **Events** — The Gunpowder Plot (1605); the 1834 fire that razed the old palace; Westminster Hall
  (1097) survives as its oldest part, scene of the trials of Charles I and Thomas More.
- **Concepts** — `gothic-revival` · `perpendicular-gothic` · `constitutional-monarchy` · `gunpowder-plot`

**Clues:**
1. The neo-Gothic seat of Parliament beside the Thames, rebuilt after a devastating fire in 1834.
2. Its clock tower, renamed the Elizabeth Tower in 2012, houses the great bell nicknamed Big Ben.
3. In 1605 Guy Fawkes was caught trying to blow up this building with barrels of gunpowder.
4. Designed by Charles Barry and Augustus Pugin, it is the definitive image of Gothic Revival Britain.
5. Home to the House of Commons and the House of Lords.

## 2 · Tower of London ★
*England · London*

**Nutshell.** A Norman fortress on the Thames begun by William the Conqueror, its White Tower raised
around 1078. For 900 years a royal castle, armoury, prison and place of execution — and still the home
of the Crown Jewels, guarded by the Yeomen Warders and its ravens.

- **When** — Norman, from 1066/1078, with medieval expansions.
- **Who** — Built by the Normans to dominate conquered London; a fortress-palace and prison for
  high-status captives.
- **People** — William the Conqueror founded it. Anne Boleyn and Catherine Howard were beheaded here;
  the "Princes in the Tower" (Edward V and his brother) vanished in 1483; Guy Fawkes was tortured here.
- **Events** — The Norman Conquest; Tudor executions; the Crown Jewels housed here since the 17th C.
- **Concepts** — `norman-conquest` · `tudor-dynasty`

**Clues:**
1. A Norman fortress begun by William the Conqueror, its White Tower dominating the Thames since the 11th century.
2. Anne Boleyn and Catherine Howard were beheaded within its walls.
3. It guards the Crown Jewels, watched over by Yeomen Warders and a flock of ravens.
4. The two young "Princes in the Tower" vanished here in 1483.
5. A royal castle, armoury and prison beside Tower Bridge for over 900 years.

## 3 · Tower Bridge ★
*England · London*

**Nutshell.** The Victorian bascule-and-suspension bridge over the Thames, completed in 1894, its two
Gothic towers and blue lifting roadway an emblem of London the world over — and often mistaken for
London Bridge.

- **When** — Victorian, opened 1894.
- **Who** — Built to ease east London's traffic while letting tall ships reach the Pool of London.
- **People** — Engineer John Wolfe Barry (son of Charles Barry) and architect Horace Jones; the towers
  clad in stone to harmonise with the neighbouring Tower of London.
- **Events** — Its bascules still lift for ships; endlessly confused with the plain London Bridge upstream.
- **Concepts** — `bascule-bridge` · `victorian` · `gothic-revival`

**Clues:**
1. A Victorian bascule bridge opened in 1894, its roadway splitting and lifting to let ships pass.
2. Its two Gothic towers were clad in stone to match the fortress just upstream.
3. Frequently confused with the plainer London Bridge nearby.
4. Designed by Horace Jones and John Wolfe Barry across the Pool of London.
5. Perhaps the most photographed river crossing in the capital.

## 4 · Buckingham Palace ★
*England · London*

**Nutshell.** The London residence and headquarters of the British monarch since Queen Victoria moved
in in 1837. Its balcony and the Changing of the Guard are focal points of national ceremony; the Royal
Standard flies when the sovereign is in.

- **When** — A Georgian townhouse of 1703 enlarged into a palace in the 19th C; the famous East Front
  dates from 1913.
- **Who** — The royal household; the working symbol of the monarchy.
- **People** — Queen Victoria, first monarch to live here (1837); architect John Nash led the enlargement.
- **Events** — Balcony appearances at coronations, jubilees and VE Day; the daily Changing of the Guard.
- **Concepts** — `constitutional-monarchy`

**Clues:**
1. The London home of the British monarch since Queen Victoria moved in in 1837.
2. Crowds gather at its gates for the Changing of the Guard.
3. The Royal Standard flies above it only when the sovereign is inside.
4. Its balcony hosts the royal family at coronations and jubilees.
5. A 775-room palace at the end of The Mall, facing the Victoria Memorial.

## 5 · St Paul's Cathedral ★
*England · London*

**Nutshell.** Sir Christopher Wren's baroque masterpiece, its great dome crowning Ludgate Hill since
1710. Built to replace the medieval cathedral lost in the Great Fire of 1666, it survived the Blitz to
become a symbol of British resilience, and has staged funerals and weddings of state.

- **When** — English Baroque, built 1675–1710 after the Great Fire of London (1666).
- **Who** — The Anglican cathedral of the Diocese of London.
- **People** — Sir Christopher Wren, architect, lies in its crypt near Nelson and Wellington.
- **Events** — The Great Fire (1666); the Blitz (1940), when the dome stood amid the smoke in a famous
  photograph; the wedding of Charles and Diana (1981); Churchill's state funeral.
- **Concepts** — `english-baroque` · `great-fire-of-london` · `the-blitz` · `church-of-england`

**Clues:**
1. Sir Christopher Wren's domed baroque cathedral, built after the Great Fire of 1666.
2. Its survival amid the flames of the 1940 Blitz became a symbol of national defiance.
3. Nelson and Wellington lie in its crypt, near Wren's own tomb.
4. Charles and Diana were married beneath its dome in 1981.
5. It crowns Ludgate Hill, the tallest building in the City for two and a half centuries.

## 6 · Westminster Abbey
*England · London*

**Nutshell.** The great Gothic church beside Parliament where English and British monarchs have been
crowned since 1066 and many are buried. A place of coronations, royal weddings and funerals, and home
to Poets' Corner.

- **When** — Gothic; largely rebuilt by Henry III from 1245 on a site founded in the 960s; west towers 18th C.
- **Who** — A "royal peculiar," answerable to the Crown rather than a bishop; the coronation church.
- **People** — Founded by Edward the Confessor (1065); coronations from William the Conqueror to Charles III;
  Newton, Darwin and Dickens buried within; Chaucer began Poets' Corner.
- **Events** — Coronations, royal weddings (William and Kate, 2011), state funerals; the Unknown Warrior's grave.
- **Concepts** — `gothic` · `coronation` · `royal-peculiar` · `poets-corner`

**Clues:**
1. Every English and British coronation since 1066 has taken place in this Gothic church.
2. It holds Poets' Corner, where Chaucer, Dickens and Hardy are buried or honoured.
3. Founded by Edward the Confessor beside the Palace of Westminster.
4. Isaac Newton and Charles Darwin lie among the monarchs beneath its floor.
5. William and Kate were married here in 2011; the Unknown Warrior rests by its west door.

## 7 · British Museum
*England · London*

**Nutshell.** The world's first national public museum, founded in 1753, telling the story of human
culture across two million years. The Rosetta Stone, the Parthenon Marbles and Egyptian mummies make it
a monument to the Enlightenment — and to unresolved debates over cultural restitution.

- **When** — Founded 1753 (opened 1759); Greek-Revival building 1820s–1850s; glass-roofed Great Court 2000.
- **Who** — A universal museum of world cultures, free to the public from the start.
- **People** — Sir Hans Sloane's bequest founded it; the Rosetta Stone let Champollion decipher hieroglyphs;
  Lord Elgin brought the contested Parthenon Marbles.
- **Events** — Enlightenment collecting and empire; today's restitution disputes (the Marbles, the Benin Bronzes).
- **Concepts** — `the-enlightenment` · `universal-museum` · `restitution` · `palladian`

**Clues:**
1. The world's first national public museum, founded in 1753 as a monument to the Enlightenment.
2. It holds the Rosetta Stone, the key that unlocked Egyptian hieroglyphs.
3. The Parthenon Marbles it displays are the subject of a long restitution dispute with Greece.
4. Its glass-roofed Great Court, opened in 2000, is Europe's largest covered square.
5. A Greek-Revival building in Bloomsbury holding two million years of human history.

## 8 · Trafalgar Square / Nelson's Column
*England · London*

**Nutshell.** London's great public square, laid out in the 1840s to commemorate Admiral Nelson's
victory — and death — at the Battle of Trafalgar (1805). Nelson's Column and its four bronze lions have
made it the city's stage for protest, celebration and New Year.

- **When** — Laid out in the 1840s; the column raised in 1843.
- **Who** — A national commemorative and gathering space, before the National Gallery.
- **People** — Admiral Horatio Nelson, killed at Trafalgar; Sir Edwin Landseer modelled the lions.
- **Events** — The Battle of Trafalgar (1805) broke the Franco-Spanish fleet in the Napoleonic Wars and
  secured British naval supremacy for a century; the square hosts rallies, VE Day and New Year.
- **Concepts** — `napoleonic-wars` · `battle-of-trafalgar`

**Clues:**
1. This London square commemorates a naval victory of 1805 over the fleets of France and Spain.
2. Its central column honours the admiral who died in the hour of that triumph.
3. Four great bronze lions guard the base of Nelson's Column.
4. Its empty "Fourth Plinth" now hosts rotating works of contemporary art.
5. London's traditional gathering place for New Year and protest, before the National Gallery.

## 9 · London Eye
*England · London*

**Nutshell.** The giant cantilevered observation wheel on the South Bank, opened for the millennium in
2000. At 135 m it was briefly the world's tallest, and quickly became a defining modern emblem of
London's skyline.

- **When** — Contemporary, opened 2000 to mark the millennium.
- **Who** — A modern icon and the city's most-visited paid attraction.
- **People** — Architects David Marks and Julia Barfield.
- **Events** — Built as a temporary millennium attraction, then made permanent.
- **Concepts** — *(modern icon; no glossary concepts)*

**Clues:**
1. A giant observation wheel raised on the South Bank to mark the year 2000.
2. At 135 m it was briefly the tallest of its kind in the world.
3. Its 32 sealed capsules turn slowly opposite the Houses of Parliament.
4. Intended as temporary, it became a permanent emblem of the skyline.
5. London's most-visited paid attraction, a modern landmark beside the Thames.

## 10 · Stonehenge ★
*England · Wiltshire*

**Nutshell.** A prehistoric ring of standing stones on Salisbury Plain, raised in stages around 2500 BC
by Neolithic and Bronze-Age Britons. Aligned to the solstices and built without metal tools or the wheel,
it is older than the Druids by some two thousand years.

- **When** — Neolithic into the Early Bronze Age; built over ~1,500 years from c. 3000 BC, the great
  sarsen circle raised c. 2500 BC.
- **Who** — Pre-Celtic, pre-literate farming communities; a ceremonial and religious site, and at first a
  cremation cemetery.
- **People** — None named: it predates written history in Britain. The Druids are popularly but wrongly
  credited — they arrived ~2,000 years too late.
- **Events** — No battle. Its colossal local sarsens and smaller bluestones — hauled ~250 km from the
  Preseli Hills of Wales — tell the story; its axis frames the solstice sun.
- **Concepts** — `neolithic` · `bronze-age` · `sarsen` · `bluestone` · `trilithon` · `henge` · `solstice`

**Clues:**
1. A ring of standing stones on Salisbury Plain, its axis aligned to the summer-solstice sunrise.
2. Its bluestones were dragged some 250 km from the Preseli Hills of Wales — by a people without the wheel.
3. Popularly tied to the Druids, this monument in fact predates them by around two thousand years.
4. Its "trilithons" pair two uprights beneath a single horizontal lintel, raised in the Neolithic without metal tools.
5. England's most famous prehistoric monument, near Amesbury in Wiltshire.

## 11 · Hadrian's Wall
*England · Cumbria–Northumberland*

**Nutshell.** A 73-mile stone frontier flung coast to coast across northern England, begun in AD 122 on
the orders of the Emperor Hadrian to mark and guard the northern edge of the Roman province of Britannia.
It is the clearest surviving monument to Rome's decision to *stop expanding* and defend fixed borders instead.

- **When** — Roman Britain, early 2nd C AD; begun AD 122, built in roughly six years.
- **Who** — Raised by Roman legionaries, garrisoned by auxiliaries; it faced the un-conquered peoples of
  the north — Caledonia, later the Picts.
- **People** — Emperor Hadrian (r. 117–138), who ordered it while visiting Britain, remembered for
  consolidating the empire's frontiers rather than enlarging them.
- **Events** — Not built for one war, but as the physical form of Hadrian's frontier strategy — a *limes*
  controlling movement, trade and taxation. His successor pushed north to the turf Antonine Wall (AD 142),
  soon abandoned.
- **Concepts** — `britannia` · `roman-province` · `limes` · `milecastle` · `caledonia-picts` · `antonine-wall`

**Clues:**
1. Begun in AD 122 on the orders of the emperor whose name it bears, marking the northern limit of Roman Britain.
2. It embodies Rome's shift from conquest to consolidation — a *limes* guarding Britannia from the tribes of Caledonia.
3. Studded with milecastles and forts such as Housesteads and Vindolanda, it runs coast to coast.
4. Twenty years on, Rome pushed north to the turf Antonine Wall — then fell back to this earlier stone line.
5. Britain's greatest Roman monument, stretching from the Solway Firth to the River Tyne.

## 12 · Roman Baths, Bath
*England · Somerset*

**Nutshell.** A remarkably preserved Roman bathing and temple complex built around Britain's only hot
springs, at Aquae Sulis — modern Bath. Here the Romans honoured a fused Celtic-Roman goddess, Sulis
Minerva; the steaming green Great Bath still stands below the Georgian city.

- **When** — Roman, built from ~AD 70 around natural hot springs (the visible upper terraces are 19th C).
- **Who** — Romano-British; a religious spa town, *Aquae Sulis*, "the waters of Sulis."
- **People** — The goddess Sulis Minerva fused the local Celtic deity Sulis with Roman Minerva — a case of
  Roman religious syncretism.
- **Events** — Pilgrims threw lead "curse tablets" into the sacred spring; Bath was reborn as a Georgian
  resort (see Royal Crescent).
- **Concepts** — `aquae-sulis` · `sulis-minerva` · `interpretatio-romana` · `curse-tablets` · `hypocaust`

**Clues:**
1. A Roman bathing complex built around Britain's only natural hot springs, at Aquae Sulis.
2. Here the Romans worshipped Sulis Minerva, a goddess fusing a Celtic deity with their own.
3. Pilgrims cast lead "curse tablets" into its sacred green spring.
4. Its steaming Great Bath lies just below the Georgian streets of Bath.
5. The best-preserved Roman religious spa in northern Europe.

## 13 · Canterbury Cathedral
*England · Kent*

**Nutshell.** The mother church of the worldwide Anglican Communion and seat of the Archbishop of
Canterbury. Founded by St Augustine in 597, it became one of Christendom's great pilgrimage sites after
Archbishop Thomas Becket was murdered here in 1170.

- **When** — Anglo-Saxon foundation (597); present Romanesque-and-Gothic church from the 11th–15th C.
- **Who** — The cradle of English Christianity and senior seat of the Church of England.
- **People** — St Augustine, sent by Pope Gregory, founded English Christianity here; Archbishop Thomas
  Becket was murdered at the altar in 1170 by knights of Henry II.
- **Events** — Augustine's mission (597); Becket's martyrdom (1170); Chaucer's pilgrims journeyed to his
  shrine; Henry VIII destroyed it at the Reformation (1538).
- **Concepts** — `anglican-communion` · `church-of-england` · `martyrdom` · `pilgrimage` · `english-reformation`

**Clues:**
1. The mother church of the Anglican Communion and seat of its senior archbishop.
2. Archbishop Thomas Becket was murdered at its altar in 1170 by knights of Henry II.
3. St Augustine founded English Christianity here in 597.
4. Chaucer's pilgrims were journeying to a shrine within these walls.
5. The spiritual heart of the Church of England, in a cathedral city of Kent.

## 14 · York Minster
*England · North Yorkshire*

**Nutshell.** The largest Gothic cathedral in northern Europe, seat of the Archbishop of York — second
only to Canterbury. Famed for the medieval stained glass of its Great East Window, it rises over a city
layered with Roman, Viking and medieval history.

- **When** — Gothic, built 1220–1472 (over 250 years) on Roman and Norman foundations.
- **Who** — The northern seat of the Church of England; its archbishop is "Primate of England."
- **People** — Constantine the Great was proclaimed emperor here in Roman York (Eboracum) in 306.
- **Events** — Roman Eboracum and Viking Jorvik lie beneath the city; a 1984 fire led to major glass
  conservation.
- **Concepts** — `gothic` · `stained-glass`

**Clues:**
1. The largest Gothic cathedral in northern Europe, seat of England's second archbishop.
2. Its Great East Window holds the largest expanse of medieval stained glass in the country.
3. It rises over a city the Romans called Eboracum and the Vikings called Jorvik.
4. Built over more than 250 years and completed in 1472.
5. The great minster of England's north, damaged by fire in 1984 and restored.

## 15 · Durham Cathedral
*England · County Durham*

**Nutshell.** A masterpiece of Norman Romanesque architecture, begun in 1093 above a bend in the River
Wear. Built to house the shrine of St Cuthbert, its ribbed stone vaults were an engineering leap towards
the Gothic; with the castle beside it, it forms a great fortress-church of the medieval north.

- **When** — Norman Romanesque, begun 1093.
- **Who** — Built by Benedictine monks; seat of the Prince-Bishops of Durham, who ruled the region almost
  as kings.
- **People** — St Cuthbert, the Northumbrian saint, is shrined here; the Venerable Bede is also buried within.
- **Events** — Norman consolidation of the north; its pioneering ribbed vaulting pointed towards Gothic.
- **Concepts** — `romanesque-norman` · `ribbed-vault` · `prince-bishop` · `pilgrimage`

**Clues:**
1. A masterpiece of Norman Romanesque, begun in 1093 above the River Wear.
2. Built to shrine St Cuthbert; the Venerable Bede also lies within.
3. Its pioneering ribbed stone vaults pointed the way towards Gothic architecture.
4. Guarded beside its castle, once the seat of prince-bishops who ruled the north like kings.
5. Northern England's great fortress-cathedral, a UNESCO site paired with Durham Castle.

## 16 · Windsor Castle ★
*England · Berkshire*

**Nutshell.** The oldest and largest inhabited castle in the world, founded by William the Conqueror and
a royal residence for over 900 years. Home to St George's Chapel and the Order of the Garter, it remains
a working home of the British monarch — and gave the royal family its very name.

- **When** — Norman origins (1070s), rebuilt over centuries; St George's Chapel is Perpendicular Gothic
  (15th–16th C).
- **Who** — A royal residence and seat of the Order of the Garter.
- **People** — William the Conqueror founded it; Henry VIII, Charles I and Elizabeth II are buried in St
  George's Chapel.
- **Events** — In 1917 the royal family renamed itself "Windsor," dropping its German title during the
  First World War; a serious fire struck in 1992.
- **Concepts** — `norman-conquest` · `order-of-the-garter` · `house-of-windsor` · `perpendicular-gothic`

**Clues:**
1. The oldest and largest inhabited castle in the world, founded by William the Conqueror.
2. In 1917 the royal family took its very name from this castle.
3. Its St George's Chapel is home to the ancient Order of the Garter.
4. Elizabeth II, Henry VIII and Charles I are all buried within its chapel.
5. A working royal residence west of London, badly damaged by fire in 1992.

## 17 · Edinburgh Castle ★
*Scotland · Edinburgh*

**Nutshell.** A fortress on an extinct volcanic crag dominating Scotland's capital. Occupied for some
3,000 years, it has been royal residence, prison and garrison, and guards the Honours of Scotland — the
oldest crown jewels in Britain — and the Stone of Destiny.

- **When** — Castle Rock fortified since the Iron Age; its oldest building, St Margaret's Chapel, dates to
  c. 1130.
- **Who** — A Scottish royal and military stronghold.
- **People** — Mary, Queen of Scots gave birth here in 1566 to the future James VI, who in 1603 also became
  James I of England, uniting the crowns.
- **Events** — Fought over repeatedly in the Wars of Scottish Independence; the One O'Clock Gun; the Honours
  of Scotland and the Stone of Destiny are kept here.
- **Concepts** — `wars-of-scottish-independence` · `honours-of-scotland` · `stone-of-destiny` · `union-of-the-crowns`

**Clues:**
1. A fortress on an extinct volcano dominating the skyline of Scotland's capital.
2. It guards the Honours of Scotland, the oldest crown jewels in the British Isles.
3. Mary, Queen of Scots bore the future James VI within its walls in 1566.
4. Its One O'Clock Gun is fired across the city every day but Sunday.
5. Repeatedly stormed during the Wars of Scottish Independence, it holds the Stone of Destiny.

## 18 · Caernarfon Castle
*Wales · Gwynedd*

**Nutshell.** A mighty fortress-palace on the Menai Strait, built by Edward I from 1283 to cement his
conquest of Wales. Its banded, polygonal towers deliberately echo imperial Constantinople; here the
English crown stages the investiture of the Prince of Wales.

- **When** — Medieval, begun 1283, the grandest of Edward I's "iron ring" of Welsh castles.
- **Who** — A symbol of the English conquest and subjugation of Wales.
- **People** — Edward I ("Longshanks"); by tradition he presented his infant son as Prince of Wales,
  origin of the title; Charles was invested here in 1969.
- **Events** — The Edwardian conquest of Wales; the "iron ring" (with Conwy, Harlech, Beaumaris); royal
  investitures.
- **Concepts** — `edward-conquest-wales` · `prince-of-wales` · `iron-ring`

**Clues:**
1. Edward I began this fortress in 1283 to seal his conquest of Wales.
2. Its banded, polygonal towers deliberately evoke the walls of imperial Constantinople.
3. The investiture of the Prince of Wales is staged within its walls.
4. The greatest of Edward's "iron ring" of Welsh castles, on the Menai Strait.
5. A symbol of English power over Wales, facing the Isle of Anglesey.

## 19 · Stirling Castle
*Scotland · Stirling*

**Nutshell.** A great fortress on a volcanic rock commanding the lowest crossing of the River Forth — "the
brooch that clasps Highlands and Lowlands." Its position made it the prize of the Wars of Scottish
Independence, fought out nearby at Stirling Bridge (1297) and Bannockburn (1314).

- **When** — Fortified from early times; the present Renaissance palace is largely 15th–16th C.
- **Who** — A Scottish royal stronghold of the Stewart kings.
- **People** — William Wallace won the Battle of Stirling Bridge (1297); Robert the Bruce won nearby
  Bannockburn (1314), securing Scottish independence.
- **Events** — The Wars of Scottish Independence: after Edward I claimed overlordship of Scotland, these
  two battles broke English power and led to independence, recognised in 1328.
- **Concepts** — `wars-of-scottish-independence` · `stirling-bridge` · `bannockburn` · `stewart-dynasty`

**Clues:**
1. A fortress guarding the lowest crossing of the Forth, called "the brooch that clasps Highlands and Lowlands."
2. William Wallace routed an English army at the bridge below it in 1297.
3. Robert the Bruce's victory at nearby Bannockburn in 1314 was won in its shadow.
4. A Renaissance palace of the Stewart kings on a volcanic crag.
5. The great strategic prize of the Wars of Scottish Independence.

## 20 · Hampton Court Palace
*England · Richmond upon Thames, London*

**Nutshell.** A vast Tudor palace on the Thames, seized by Henry VIII from Cardinal Wolsey and enlarged
into a pleasure-palace of Tudor power. Later half-rebuilt in English baroque for William and Mary, it pairs
Henry's Great Hall and haunted galleries with Wren's state apartments and a famous maze.

- **When** — Tudor (from 1515) and English Baroque (Wren's wing, 1690s).
- **Who** — A royal palace of the Tudor and Stuart courts.
- **People** — Cardinal Thomas Wolsey built it, then lost it to Henry VIII when he failed to secure the
  king's annulment; the ghost of Catherine Howard is said to haunt a gallery.
- **Events** — Wolsey's fall fed into the English Reformation; the 1604 Hampton Court Conference set in
  motion the King James Bible.
- **Concepts** — `tudor-dynasty` · `cardinal-wolsey` · `english-reformation` · `king-james-bible` · `english-baroque`

**Clues:**
1. Cardinal Wolsey built this Thames-side palace, then lost it to Henry VIII.
2. A Tudor pleasure-palace whose Great Hall and maze survive beside a later baroque wing by Wren.
3. The ghost of Catherine Howard is said to haunt its gallery.
4. A conference here in 1604 set in motion the King James Bible.
5. The grandest surviving Tudor palace, upstream from central London.

## 21 · Radcliffe Camera, Oxford
*England · Oxfordshire*

**Nutshell.** The domed, circular library at the heart of Oxford — the oldest university in the
English-speaking world — and the emblem of its "dreaming spires." Built in the 1740s in the English
Palladian style, it is the reading room around which the ancient colleges cluster.

- **When** — Georgian, 1737–1749; English Palladian / neo-classical.
- **Who** — The University of Oxford, teaching since around 1096.

## Glossary

Shared, linked concept layer. Definitions kept concise; reused across entries.

### Periods & dynasties
- **neolithic** — Neolithic (New Stone Age): final Stone-Age phase, c. 4000–2500 BC in Britain — farming, polished-stone tools and monument-building, before metalworking.
- **bronze-age** — c. 2500–800 BC in Britain: the arrival of metal, copper then bronze, for tools and weapons.
- **norman-conquest** — The 1066 conquest of England by William, Duke of Normandy, replacing the Anglo-Saxon ruling class and reshaping law, language and castle-building.
- **tudor-dynasty** — The royal house of 1485–1603 (Henry VII to Elizabeth I), spanning the English Reformation and the Elizabethan golden age.
- **stewart-dynasty** — The Scottish (later British) royal house of the Stewarts/Stuarts, ruling Scotland from 1371 and Britain 1603–1714.
- **elizabethan-jacobean** — The reigns of Elizabeth I (1558–1603) and James I (1603–1625): the age of Shakespeare and English Renaissance drama.
- **georgian** — 1714–1830, the reigns of Kings George I–IV (House of Hanover): elegant, symmetrical classical architecture. → Royal Crescent, Radcliffe Camera.
- **regency** — 1811–1820, when George IV ruled as Prince Regent during his father's illness; a refined sub-period of the Georgian, the world of Jane Austen.
- **victorian** — 1837–1901, the reign of Queen Victoria: the industrial age, empire, and ornate Gothic Revival. → Tower Bridge, Clifton Bridge.
- **edwardian** — 1901–1910, the reign of Edward VII: lighter baroque-revival grandeur, the calm before the First World War. → Titanic.

### Architecture & styles
- **romanesque-norman** — Heavy round-arched stone architecture brought by the Normans (11th–12th C). → Durham.
- **gothic** — The pointed-arch, soaring, light-filled style of c. 1150–1500. → Westminster Abbey, York Minster.
- **perpendicular-gothic** — The last, distinctly English phase of Gothic (late 14th–16th C): strong vertical lines, huge windows, fan vaults. → King's College Chapel, St George's Chapel.
- **gothic-revival** — The 19th-C romantic revival of medieval Gothic. → Palace of Westminster, Tower Bridge.
- **english-baroque** — The grand, classical, dome-and-column style of c. 1666–1720. → St Paul's, Hampton Court (Wren wing).
- **palladian** — Restrained classical architecture after Andrea Palladio, dominant in Georgian Britain: symmetry, columns, proportion. → Royal Crescent, Radcliffe Camera.
- **timber-framed** — "Half-timbered" building of exposed wooden frame with infill, typical of Tudor houses. → Shakespeare's Birthplace.
- **fan-vault** — A ceiling of fan-shaped ribs radiating from slender supports; the signature of Perpendicular Gothic. → King's College Chapel.
- **ribbed-vault** — A stone ceiling framed by projecting ribs; pioneered at Durham, it opened the way to Gothic.
- **trilithon** — Two upright stones carrying a horizontal lintel (Greek, "three stones"). → Stonehenge.
- **bascule-bridge** — A drawbridge whose counterweighted sections tilt upward to let ships pass. → Tower Bridge.
- **suspension-bridge** — A bridge whose deck hangs from cables slung between towers. → Clifton.
- **hypocaust** — The Roman under-floor system that circulated hot air to heat baths and rooms.
- **terrace-crescent** — A unified row (or curved arc) of houses behind one continuous façade — Georgian town planning. → Royal Crescent.

### Events, wars & movements
- **gunpowder-plot** — The 1605 Catholic conspiracy, foiled when Guy Fawkes was caught trying to blow up Parliament; marked each 5 November.
- **great-fire-of-london** — The 1666 fire that destroyed much of the medieval City, clearing the way for Wren's St Paul's.
- **the-blitz** — The German bombing of British cities in 1940–41; St Paul's survival became a symbol of defiance.
- **wars-of-the-roses** — The 15th-C dynastic civil wars between the houses of Lancaster and York, ended by the Tudors in 1485.
- **english-reformation** — Henry VIII's 1530s break from Rome, creating the Church of England and dissolving the monasteries.
- **wars-of-scottish-independence** — The late 13th–14th C wars in which Scotland resisted English overlordship; Wallace and Bruce secured independence, recognised in 1328.
- **stirling-bridge** — Battle of 1297: William Wallace's victory over an English army at the Forth crossing.
- **bannockburn** — Battle of 1314: Robert the Bruce's decisive defeat of Edward II, sealing Scottish independence.
- **edward-conquest-wales** — Edward I's conquest of Wales (1277–1283), locked in by his "iron ring" of castles.
- **napoleonic-wars** — The 1803–1815 wars against Napoleon's France; Britain's naval and later land victories (Trafalgar, Waterloo) defined the age.
- **battle-of-trafalgar** — 1805 naval battle off Spain; Nelson destroyed the Franco-Spanish fleet and died, securing a century of British sea power.
- **the-enlightenment** — The 17th–18th C movement prizing reason, science and universal knowledge. → British Museum.
- **industrial-revolution** — Britain's c. 1760–1840 transformation by machines, iron and steam, the first in the world. → Clifton Bridge.
- **romanticism** — The late-18th/19th-C movement exalting emotion, nature and the sublime. → Lake District.
- **wwii-home-front** — Britain's wartime civilian experience (1939–45): the Blitz, evacuation, and symbols like the White Cliffs.

### Monarchy, church & institutions
- **constitutional-monarchy** — A monarchy whose powers are limited by law and Parliament; the sovereign reigns but does not rule.
- **coronation** — The ceremony crowning a monarch; held at Westminster Abbey since 1066.
- **order-of-the-garter** — The senior order of chivalry, founded c. 1348, based at St George's Chapel, Windsor.
- **house-of-windsor** — The reigning royal house, renamed from Saxe-Coburg-Gotha in 1917 to shed its German title in wartime.
- **union-of-the-crowns** — 1603, when James VI of Scotland also became James I of England, uniting the two crowns under one king.
- **prince-of-wales** — Title given to the heir to the English/British throne since Edward I; conferred by investiture at Caernarfon.
- **honours-of-scotland** — The Scottish crown jewels (crown, sceptre, sword) — the oldest in Britain — kept at Edinburgh Castle.
- **stone-of-destiny** — The ancient sandstone block on which Scottish (and later British) monarchs were enthroned.
- **prince-bishop** — A bishop wielding secular as well as spiritual power over a territory; the Bishops of Durham ruled a near-independent palatinate.
- **royal-peculiar** — A church answerable directly to the Crown, outside the authority of any bishop. → Westminster Abbey.
- **poets-corner** — The area of Westminster Abbey where great writers are buried or memorialised.
- **anglican-communion** — The worldwide family of churches descended from the Church of England, led spiritually by the Archbishop of Canterbury.
- **church-of-england** — The established Protestant church of England, born of the Reformation, with the monarch as Supreme Governor.
- **cardinal-wolsey** — Thomas Wolsey (c. 1473–1530), Henry VIII's chief minister, who fell after failing to win the king's annulment.
- **king-james-bible** — The 1611 English translation of the Bible, set in motion at the 1604 Hampton Court Conference; hugely influential on the language.
- **collegiate-university** — A university made of semi-autonomous residential colleges. → Oxford, Cambridge.
- **bodleian** — Oxford's historic research library, one of the oldest in Europe.
- **universal-museum** — A museum collecting the whole span of world cultures under one roof. → British Museum.
- **restitution** — The debate over returning cultural objects to their places of origin (e.g. the Parthenon Marbles).
- **public-art** — Art made for open public space. → Angel of the North.
- **martyrdom** — Death suffered for one's faith; Becket's murder made Canterbury a pilgrimage site.
- **pilgrimage** — A journey to a sacred place; medieval England's great routes led to shrines like Becket's and Cuthbert's.
- **dreaming-spires** — Matthew Arnold's phrase for Oxford's skyline of towers and spires.

### Roman Britain
- **britannia** — The Roman province covering most of present-day England and Wales, from the conquest of AD 43.
- **roman-province** — A territory outside Italy governed by Rome under an appointed governor.
- **limes** — The fortified, defended frontier line of the Roman Empire. → Hadrian's Wall.
- **milecastle** — A small guarded fortlet set at roughly every Roman mile along Hadrian's Wall.
- **caledonia-picts** — The peoples of what is now Scotland, north of the Wall, never conquered by Rome.
- **antonine-wall** — A later turf-and-timber frontier built ~160 km further north under Antoninus Pius (AD 142), soon abandoned.
- **aquae-sulis** — "The waters of Sulis," the Roman name for Bath and its sacred hot springs.
- **sulis-minerva** — The goddess of the Bath spring, fusing the Celtic Sulis with the Roman Minerva.
- **interpretatio-romana** — The Roman habit of identifying foreign gods with their own — religious syncretism.
- **curse-tablets** — Inscribed lead sheets (*defixiones*) thrown into the Bath spring to invoke the goddess against wrongdoers.

### Geography, nature & myth
- **sarsen** — Hard local sandstone; the largest Stonehenge stones, dragged ~30 km from the Marlborough Downs.
- **bluestone** — Smaller igneous Stonehenge stones brought ~250 km from the Preseli Hills of west Wales.
- **henge** — A prehistoric circular earthwork of bank and ditch; Stonehenge is atypical, its ditch lying outside the bank.
- **solstice** — The year's longest (summer) and shortest (winter) days, marking the sun's extremes on the horizon.
- **stained-glass** — Coloured window glass telling stories in light; York holds England's largest medieval expanse.
- **basalt-columns** — Hexagonal pillars formed as thick lava cools and contracts. → Giant's Causeway.
- **chalk-geology** — Soft white limestone of compressed marine microfossils, forming the cliffs of southern England.
- **the-english-channel** — The sea separating England from France, ~33 km wide at the Strait of Dover.
- **great-glen** — The straight fault-line valley slicing across the Scottish Highlands, holding Loch Ness.
- **the-highlands** — The mountainous northern region of Scotland.
- **cryptozoology** — The search for animals whose existence is unproven, such as the Loch Ness Monster.
- **glaciation** — The Ice-Age carving of valleys and lakes by moving ice, shaping the Lakes, Snowdonia and the Highlands.
- **eryri** — The Welsh name for Snowdonia, the mountainous national park around Yr Wyddfa.
- **welsh-identity** — Welsh language and national culture; the restoring of names like Yr Wyddfa is one expression.
- **lake-poets** — Wordsworth, Coleridge and Southey, the Romantic poets of the Lake District.
- **the-sublime** — The 18th-C aesthetic of awe and grandeur in wild nature, central to Romanticism.
- **fionn-mac-cumhaill** — Finn McCool, the giant-hero of Irish myth, said to have built the Giant's Causeway.
- **harland-and-wolff** — The Belfast shipyard that built RMS Titanic; its cranes still mark the skyline.
- **iron-ring** — Edward I's chain of castles (Caernarfon, Conwy, Harlech, Beaumaris) built to hold conquered Wales.
- **king-james-bible** *(see Monarchy, church & institutions)*
